Hospital Triage: a full emergency flow, from the front desk to the waiting-room screen

An emergency management system built on the Manchester Triage Protocol, the same protocol that orders Portuguese emergency departments. Two disciplines in one design: clinical practice sets the rules, software makes them executable. Whoever walks in is admitted, triaged by symptoms, ordered by clinical priority and called to the consultation room. Each role on the team sees only what belongs to it.

The five Manchester colours and their target times run through the whole system, from triage to the waiting-room display.

The patient's journey

The system follows the emergency episode from start to finish, backed by a state machine: admitted → triaged → in consultation → outcome.

  1. AdmissionArrival registration with national health number validation. A dedicated emergency entry for patients arriving unconscious or without identification: the episode opens first, identity is resolved later.
  2. Nursing triageSelectable symptoms and vital signs (blood pressure, heart rate, temperature, SpO₂). Priority is computed by the Manchester engine; the nurse confirms it, never invents it.
  3. Waiting queueOrdered by colour and time, with counters per priority and target times in sight. Refreshes on its own every 30 seconds.
  4. Doctor's panel"Call next" follows clinical priority, not order of arrival. Clinical notes per episode and closure with a recorded outcome.
  5. Waiting roomThe public display closes the loop: patients see the ticket and the room, the team sees the state of the department.

Back-office functions

  • Patient management: record, search, visit history and validated editing.
  • Management reports: patients per priority, average waiting time and "left without being seen" (a real emergency-department indicator), by date range, with CSV export.
  • User management: four profiles with distinct access: Admin, Doctor, Nurse and Clerk. Each one logs in and sees only its own panels.

The mechanisms underneath

  • MVC architecture with DAOs: model, view and controllers kept apart; data access isolated in UtenteDAO and UtilizadorDAO.
  • Two SQLite databases, on purpose: credentials in one file, clinical data in another. Stealing one does not give you the other.
  • Passwords with scrypt: salted hashes, resistant to brute force; never in plain text.
  • Episode state machine: ADMITTED → TRIAGED → IN_CONSULTATION → outcome; every transition has rules.
  • Priority engine: critical symptoms pull the colour up; the combination decides, not opinion.
  • Check-digit validators: tax number, national health number and phone verified mathematically, not by eye.
  • Role-based access control (RBAC): the profile builds the interface: a clerk never sees the doctor's panel.

Possibilities

Networked multi-stationAdmission, triage and consultation rooms on different machines over the same database; the DAO design already allows it.
A real TV in the waiting roomThe public display was born for a large screen; all that is missing is the HDMI cable.
Management indicatorsTimes per colour, per shift, per season: the reports are the seed of an emergency management dashboard.
Target-time alertsAn "urgent" patient past the 60-minute mark should raise an alert. The engine already knows the times; only the alarm is missing.
Other clinical queuesHealth centres, private clinics, veterinary practices: wherever there is clinical priority and a waiting room, the flow fits.
Local-first by natureClinical data lives on the machine, not in a third-party cloud: privacy by architecture, not by promise.
